August 5, 2013, 4:09

By Carole Kelleher Special to the Index Tribune

Jerry Caine has run through driving rain. He has run in the snow and in 120-degree heat.

He has run through earthquakes and on cruise ships.

He ran on his wedding day and on days when he was in the hospital.

Runner Jerry Caine

He ran yesterday, and he will probably run today.

Caine, 63, has run every day for the past 22 years.

“It's like breathing,” said the Santa Rosa substitute teacher. “I don't think about it. It's not a question of if I'm going to run today, it's a question of when and how far.”

Caine has one of the longer active running streaks in the country, according to the United States Running Streak Association, which actually keeps track of this unusual pursuit.

Caine recently moved into 93rd place on the list after the reigning record-holder, Mark Covert of Lancaster, ceremoniously ended his streak at exactly 45 years on June 23.

“Everyone has their own reason why they run every day,” said Mark Washburne, president of the 500-member USRSA. “We like to call ourselves dedicated.”

The organization's only rule is that a streak-holder must run at least one continuous mile within each calendar day without any mechanical aid. Reporting is on the honor system.

There have been a few days when Caine has run the minimum one mile, but he typically runs much more. His biggest day was 90 miles, and he has recently averaged 61 miles per week.

Caine has meticulously logged each of his 121,000 miles, enough to circle Earth nearly 5 times, by hand in annotated notebooks.

Poring over his notes at his Rincon Valley house one recent morning, Caine, tanned and thin with short gray hair and glasses, recalled days when he almost broke the streak that began October 2, 1990.

In 1994, he needed an operation for a hernia. The surgery was scheduled for 7 a.m., so he woke up at 2 a.m. to get in his run. Though his doctor told him to rest for a month, he was back pounding the pavement the following evening.
